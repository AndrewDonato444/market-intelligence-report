# Persona: The Report Reader (Primary — Report Content)

> This persona governs **what goes into the report PDF**, not the app UI.
> The 5 app-level personas (Alex, Jordan, Taylor, Pat, Morgan) drive UI/UX.
> This persona drives report tone, density, vocabulary, and section design.

## Profile

- **Who**: A luxury real estate agent (solo or team lead), 5-30 years experience
- **Market**: $5M+ residential, any geography
- **Moment of use**: Preparing for a listing presentation, client advisory call, or team briefing
- **Emotional state when opening**: Slightly anxious — they need to sound smart in a room full of money

## The 3 Feelings This Report Must Trigger

### 1. "I couldn't have done this on my own."
The report must contain analysis, indexes, and cross-referenced insights that no single agent could assemble manually. Not just data — *synthesized intelligence*. Named properties as proof points, segment-level trends that contradict the headline number, timing signals that require computation.

**What delivers this**: Proprietary indexes (Market Insights Index, segment scorecards), per-segment YoY that tells a different story than the market average, neighborhood-level micro-trends, forward projections with confidence ranges.

**What kills this**: Generic stats available on Zillow. One-number summaries. Identical ratings across segments. Blank/N/A fields.

### 2. "If I wanted to do this myself, it would cost me a fortune."
The report must *look and feel* institutional. Not a flyer. Not a blog post. The density of analysis, the number of data points cross-referenced, the editorial voice — all of it should signal "this came from a research team, not a template."

**What delivers this**: Multi-source data (transaction data + news + forecasts + demographics), methodology transparency, confidence ratings, contradiction flagging, 3-scenario forecasts.

**What kills this**: Thin sections with padding. Repeated insights across sections. Obvious template language. Sections that say nothing ("the market is dynamic and evolving").

### 3. "I can actually use this to land more clients."
The report must contain *quotable moments* — a stat, a framing, a forecast that the agent can drop into a conversation and sound like the smartest person in the room. Every section should produce at least one "did you know..." moment.

**What delivers this**: Pull quotes with specific numbers. Contrarian insights ("while the overall market dropped 36%, the $20M+ segment actually grew 8%"). Named-property proof points ("the Bel Air estate at X reduced from $45M to $38M — third major reduction this quarter"). Clear buy/wait/sell signals.

**What kills this**: Hedged language with no stance. Abstract analysis with no specifics. Sections that inform but don't arm.

## What They Care About

- **Credibility on demand** — can I open this report to any page and find something worth saying?
- **Differentiation** — does this make me look different from every other agent in the room?
- **Recency** — is this clearly current, not warmed-over data?
- **Specificity** — named neighborhoods, named properties, specific numbers — not generalities

## What Frustrates Them

- Blank fields / N/A metrics — instantly destroys confidence in the whole report
- All segments showing the same number — "this is clearly broken"
- Zip codes instead of neighborhood names — "my clients don't talk in zip codes"
- Generic language that could apply to any market — "I could have written this"
- Sections that repeat the same insight in different words
- Reports that are long but not dense — padding is obvious and insulting
- Hedging without conviction — "the market may or may not..."

## Their Vocabulary

When reading the report, they think in these terms:
- "intelligence brief" not "market report"
- "market posture" not "market conditions"
- "conviction" not "opinion"
- "positioning" not "pricing"
- "advisory" not "sales"
- "UHNW clients" or "principals" not "buyers"
- Neighborhood names (Brentwood, Bel Air) not zip codes (90049, 90077)

## Patience Level

**Medium** — They'll read a 20-page report cover to cover IF every page earns its place. They won't read 30 pages where 12 are persona padding. Dense and useful beats long and comprehensive. They'll skip a section the moment it feels like filler.

## Success Metric

"Did I walk into the meeting feeling like the most informed person in the room — and did the client feel it too?"

## Report Design Implications

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Every metric must have a value** | No N/A fields. If data is unavailable, omit the metric entirely rather than showing a blank. |
| **Every section must have a quotable moment** | At least one specific, surprising, or contrarian insight per section. |
| **Segments must tell different stories** | If all segments show the same number, the segmentation is broken. |
| **Neighborhoods must have names** | Always human-readable. "Brentwood" not "90049". |
| **Density over length** | 18 pages of insight beats 30 pages with padding. |
| **Stance over hedge** | Take a position. "Buyer's market" not "market conditions are mixed." |
| **Proof points over abstractions** | Reference specific properties, specific price reductions, specific timelines. |
